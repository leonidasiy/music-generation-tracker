Research Directions & Undergraduate Suitability

🔥 Hot Directions (active frontier)

1. Multi-track / stem orchestration

Biggest gap between academic models and real production. JEN-1 Composer, SyncTrack, SketchSong all in last 18 months. Whoever solves coherent multi-stem wins.

Hot 🔥
2. Long-form coherent structure

Going from 30s clips to songs with verse/chorus/bridge. Hierarchical approach (plan→fill) is winning. Open models still can't reliably do 3-min songs.

Hot 🔥
3. Instruction tuning for music

FM Survey calls this underexplored. Everyone does text-to-music; nobody can follow instructions like "make the bass louder in chorus 2." Solved in LLMs, unsolved in music.

Hot 🔥
4. Evaluation crisis

FAD is broken. Human eval doesn't scale. SongBench (2026), Music Arena (2025) are trying. Meta-problem blocking all other progress.

Hot 🔥

🌊 Growing Directions

  • Non-autoregressive / efficient generation — MAGNeT shows parallel decoding works. Real-time on-device is the endgame.
  • Symbolic + audio joint models — MIDI-LLaMA, MuPT handle symbolic; nobody bridges symbolic+audio convincingly.
  • Disentangled control — IRCAM separates pitch/timing from genre/timbre. Dream: independent sliders for every musical dimension.

Refined research agenda · July 2026

Long-form structure × music instruction following

The defensible bridge is section-level compositional plan adherence: whether generated music realizes requested sections, entries, exits, repetitions, variations and global trajectories while remaining coherent.

Central question: Can we automatically and diagnostically evaluate whether generated music follows a requested section-level compositional and orchestration plan, while distinguishing structural development from looping and separating adherence from musical quality?

Executive verdict

STRONGConceptual connection

Long-form structure and instruction following meet naturally when instructions specify a temporal musical plan.

LOW NOVELTYGeneric coherence + alignment score

SongEval, SongBench, MusicEval and CMI-RewardBench already occupy adjacent broad evaluation territory.

PROMISINGDiagnostic section-level benchmark

Temporal order, negation, recurrence-with-variation and wrong-section violations remain much more defensible targets.

CONDITIONALPrompt-engineering follow-up

Cheap to test, but precise temporal control may require architecture-level conditioning rather than better wording.

Recommended Paper 1

Working title: Evaluating Section-Level Arrangement and Instrumentation Control in Text-to-Music Systems

Scope

  • Instrumental audio first
  • 30–90 second core examples
  • Small multi-minute stress-test subset
  • Arrangement and instrumentation controls
  • Three to four generator families

Instruction ontology

  • Instrument presence and absence
  • Section order and boundaries
  • Instrument entry and exit
  • Density and dynamics trajectory
  • Repetition versus variation
  • Negation and temporal relations
Do not build one “magic score.” Report a diagnostic vector. A track can obey a prompt while sounding poor, or sound excellent while ignoring the requested plan.

Metric suite

Atomic compliance

Score every requested constraint independently: presence, absence, timing, section location, count, order and recurrence.

Form agreement

Compare requested and estimated boundaries, section sequences and affinity graphs; penalize missing, reordered and duplicated sections.

Recurrence vs. looping

Reward recognizable thematic return with controlled variation while penalizing exact duplication and unrelated drift.

Temporal localization

Evaluate attributes inside their requested interval and penalize evidence in forbidden or incorrect sections.

Intrinsic coherence

Keep transition plausibility, late-song drift, truncation and global trajectory separate from prompt adherence.

Quality controls

Measure artifacts, musicality, production quality and listener preference independently to expose trade-offs.

Controlled violations

The benchmark should include known counterfactual failures rather than relying only on naturally generated mistakes.

Structural

  • Swap two sections
  • Delete the final return
  • Duplicate one section exactly
  • Replace a reprise with unrelated material
  • Truncate the outro

Instructional

  • Move an instrument entry to the wrong section
  • Insert a prohibited instrument
  • Flatten a requested crescendo
  • Reverse two requested events
  • Preserve concepts but scramble their order

Falsifiable hypotheses

  1. Section-local evaluation outperforms global text–audio similarity on wrong-section violations.
  2. Atomic scoring outperforms whole-prompt scoring on negation, order and multi-constraint prompts.
  3. A recurrence-development score distinguishes purposeful reprises from exact loops.
  4. Adherence and intrinsic coherence are empirically separable.
  5. Metric gains persist on an unseen generator and unseen prompt templates.

Two-stage roadmap

Two-week feasibility pilot

Use two models and a small balanced prompt set. Confirm that listeners agree, detectors are usable and existing metrics fail on at least one important counterfactual.

Paper 1: benchmark and meta-evaluation

Freeze the ontology, controlled violations and metric components; validate against humans with held-out generator and prompt-family tests.

Paper 2A: prompt-only intervention

Compare prose, keywords, constraint lists, section-tagged prompts and optimized paraphrases across fixed generators.

Paper 2B: inference-time optimization

Try best-of-N selection, reranking, prompt search and constraint-specific regeneration before training anything.

Optional lightweight adapter

Only if prompt-only methods plateau: add one section or instrumentation control through a small adapter or LoRA.

Go / no-go gates

Proceed when

  • Human judgments have usable reliability
  • Existing baselines fail on meaningful distinctions
  • The metric generalizes to an unseen generator
  • Results survive control for audio quality

Stop or rescope when

  • Detector noise dominates the labels
  • Listeners cannot agree on the constructs
  • Only system-level—not item-level—ranking improves
  • Paper 2 improves only the new metric, not blind human judgments
